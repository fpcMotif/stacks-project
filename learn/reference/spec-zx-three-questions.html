Three questions about $\operatorname{Spec}$, answered visually

The three questions that make $\operatorname{Spec}$ click — drawn, then fully explained. Each builds directly on Lesson 0001 (points $=$ primes, $V(I)$, $D(f)$, generic/closed points, residue fields).

① Draw me $\operatorname{Spec}\,\mathbb Z[x]$

This is Mumford's arithmetic surface — the friendliest 2-dimensional spectrum. Because $\mathbb Z[x]$ is a UFD, its height-1 primes are exactly the principal primes $(g)$ with $g$ irreducible, and there are only two kinds of irreducible element — so only two kinds of "curve."

② Why isn't $\operatorname{Spec}(R)$ Hausdorff?

Hausdorff means any two distinct points sit in disjoint open neighborhoods. The Zariski topology has far too few opens for that — for two reasons, both visible already in $\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z$.

Pinning down the two reasons:

  • Points need not be closed. Hausdorff $\Rightarrow$ T1 (every point closed). But the generic point $(0)$ of a domain has closure the whole space — and $(0)\in D(f)$ for every $f\neq0$, so it sits inside every nonempty open. You can never wall it off.
  • Nonempty opens always meet. For an irreducible $\operatorname{Spec}(R)$ (e.g. $R$ a domain), $D(f)\cap D(g)=D(fg)\neq\varnothing$ whenever $f,g\neq0$. In $\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z$ the only proper closed sets are finite sets of primes, so every nonempty open is cofinite, and two cofinite sets always intersect.
$\operatorname{Spec}(R)$ is Hausdorff $\;\iff\;$ it is $T_1$ $\;\iff\;$ every prime is maximal $\;\iff\;$ $\dim R = 0$.

So the only Hausdorff spectra are the dimension-0 ones: a field gives a single point; an Artinian ring gives a finite discrete set; a Boolean ring gives a Cantor-like Stone space. Everything with actual geometric dimension is not Hausdorff — and that's by design. The Zariski topology is deliberately coarse; the geometric richness is moved into the structure sheaf $\mathcal O_X$ instead. Generic points aren't a bug — they let you speak of "the behavior at the generic point," i.e. the function field.

③ What's the residue field at $(p)$?

An element $f\in R$ behaves like a function on $\operatorname{Spec}(R)$. Its value at a point $\mathfrak p$ lives in the residue field

$\kappa(\mathfrak p)\;=\;\operatorname{Frac}(R/\mathfrak p)\;=\;R_{\mathfrak p}/\mathfrak p R_{\mathfrak p}$, \quad and \quad $f(\mathfrak p):=\big[f\big]\in\kappa(\mathfrak p)$.
Try it: evaluate the integer $n$ at the point $(p)$.

The pattern generalizes: closed points carry small (finite) residue fields; generic points carry big transcendental ones. The local ring $R_{\mathfrak p}$ reveals the dimension through its residue field. Reading it off the arithmetic surface from Q1:

Point$\kappa(\mathfrak p)$flavor
$(0)$ — generic, dim 2$\mathbb Q(x)$function field, transcendental
$(p)$ — vertical line$\mathbb F_p(x)$function field over $\mathbb F_p$
$(f)$ — horizontal curve$\operatorname{Frac}(\mathbb Z[x]/(f))$a number field
$(p,f)$ — closed point$\mathbb F_{p^{\deg \bar f}}$a finite field

And here Q1 and Q3 fuse: at the closed point $(p,x^2+1)$ over an inert prime the residue field is $\mathbb F_{p^2}$, while over a split prime the two points each have residue field $\mathbb F_p$ — the residue field is the splitting type.
