$\operatorname{Spec}\,k[x,y]$: the affine plane $\mathbb A^2_k$

The geometric 2-dimensional spectrum — the natural twin of the arithmetic surface $\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z[x]$. Take $k$ algebraically closed; then the picture is exactly the classical plane, upgraded with generic points.

The three kinds of point

$k[x,y]$ is a 2-dimensional UFD, so its primes come in three heights:

Prime of $k[x,y]$Geometryheight / dim$\kappa(\mathfrak p)$
$(0)$the whole plane (its generic point)0 / dim 2$k(x,y)$
$(f)$, $f$ irreduciblean irreducible curve $\{f=0\}$1 / dim 1$\operatorname{Frac}\!\big(k[x,y]/(f)\big)$
$(x-a,\,y-b)$the point $(a,b)$2 / dim 0$k$
Why $k=\bar k$ matters The clean "maximal ideals $=$ points $(a,b)$" is exactly the Nullstellensatz, and it needs $k$ algebraically closed. Over $\mathbb R$, e.g., $(x^2+1)$ is also maximal — extra closed points with residue field $\mathbb C$, just like the arithmetic case.

Specialization: dimension drops along closures

Two ways to be a surface

$\operatorname{Spec} k[x,y]$ and $\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z[x]$ are both 2-dimensional, but they get there differently — one geometric, one arithmetic:

$k[x,y]$ — geometric$\mathbb Z[x]$ — arithmetic
base direction$\mathbb A^1_k=\operatorname{Spec} k[x]$$\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z$ (the primes)
closed points$(a,b)$, residue field $k$$(p,f)$, residue field $\mathbb F_{p^d}$

generic point$\kappa=k(x,y)$$\kappa=\mathbb Q(x)$

This analogy — number fields behaving like function fields of curves — is one of the deep organizing ideas of modern arithmetic geometry.
