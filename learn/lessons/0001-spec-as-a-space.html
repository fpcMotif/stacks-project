$\operatorname{Spec}(R)$: from a ring to a space

Win: turn any ring into a topological space & read off its points Time: ~15 min Needs: prime ideals, radicals, localization Ref: Glossary

You already know the algebra of a commutative ring $R$ — primes, radicals, localization. This lesson installs the one idea that converts all of that knowledge into geometry: the spectrum $\operatorname{Spec}(R)$. Everything in scheme theory is built on top of this single move, so it's the right first brick.1

1. Primary reference: Stacks Project, Algebra § "The spectrum of a ring," Tag 00DZ.

1 · The points are the primes

Definition — the spectrum $\operatorname{Spec}(R)$ is the set of prime ideals of $R$. Each prime $\mathfrak p$ is a point of the space.2
2. Tag 00DZ. Recall a prime $\mathfrak p\neq R$ satisfies $ab\in\mathfrak p\Rightarrow a\in\mathfrak p$ or $b\in\mathfrak p$.

Why primes and not, say, maximal ideals? Because we want the construction to be functorial: a ring map $\varphi\colon R\to S$ should give a map of spaces. It does — $\mathfrak q\mapsto \varphi^{-1}(\mathfrak q)$ — precisely because the preimage of a prime is always prime. (Preimages of maximal ideals need not be maximal.)3 Note the arrow reverses: $\operatorname{Spec}$ is contravariant.

3. Functoriality of $\operatorname{Spec}$: Tag 00E2.
Intuition — a "point" is a way of evaluating Think of elements of $R$ as functions on the space. At a point $\mathfrak p$, the value of $f$ lives in the residue field $\kappa(\mathfrak p)=\operatorname{Frac}(R/\mathfrak p)$, and "$f(\mathfrak p)=0$" means exactly $f\in\mathfrak p$. A prime ideal is the set of functions vanishing at that point — that's why primes deserve to be called points.

2 · The closed sets are $V(I)$

Definition — vanishing sets For an ideal $I\subseteq R$, $\;V(I)=\{\mathfrak p\in\operatorname{Spec}(R)\mid I\subseteq\mathfrak p\}$ — the points where "every function in $I$ vanishes." The Zariski topology declares the sets $V(I)$ to be exactly the closed sets.4
4. These really form a topology (arbitrary $\cap$, finite $\cup$, $\emptyset$ and the whole space): Tag 00E0.

Two facts make this a faithful dictionary back to algebra:

  • Only the radical matters: $V(I)=V(\sqrt I)$, since $\mathfrak p\supseteq I \iff \mathfrak p\supseteq\sqrt I$. So radical ideals $\leftrightarrow$ closed sets, order-reversingly.5
  • Inclusion reverses: $I\subseteq J \Rightarrow V(I)\supseteq V(J)$. More constraints = fewer points.
5. $V(I)=V(\sqrt I)$ and the closed-set calculus: Tag 00E5.
Definition — distinguished opens $D(f)=\{\mathfrak p\mid f\notin\mathfrak p\}=\operatorname{Spec}(R)\setminus V(f)$, the locus where $f$ is "nonzero." These form a basis for the topology, and crucially $D(f)\cong\operatorname{Spec}(R_f)$: localizing the ring = restricting to an open.6
6. $D(f)$ a basis & $D(f)\cong\operatorname{Spec}(R_f)$: Tag 00E4. Also: $\operatorname{Spec}(R)$ is always quasi-compact, Tag 00E8.

3 · Two kinds of point: closed vs. generic

The Zariski topology is not Hausdorff — points need not be closed, and that's a feature.

Point typeWhich primeClosure $\overline{\{\mathfrak p\}}=V(\mathfrak p)$
closed pointmaximal ideal $\mathfrak m$just itself
generic pointminimal prime (e.g. $(0)$ in a domain)a whole irreducible component

For a domain, $(0)$ is prime and its closure is the entire space: a single generic point whose "smear" is everywhere.7 Irreducible closed sets correspond bijectively to their unique generic points — geometry and algebra locked together.

7. Irreducible ↔ generic point: Tag 00ES.

4 · See it: four spectra

$R$$\operatorname{Spec}(R)$Shape
a field $k$one point $(0)$a dot
$\mathbb Z$generic $(0)$ + one closed point $(p)$ per prime $p$"arithmetic line"
$\mathbb C[x]$generic $(0)$ + closed $(x-a)$ for each $a\in\mathbb C$the affine line $\mathbb A^1$
$k[x]/(x^2)$one point $(x)$ — but with nilpotentsa "fat" point

The last one is the payoff of using all primes and remembering the ring: $k[x]/(x^2)$ and $k$ have the same single point but are different schemes — the nilpotent $x$ records an infinitesimal "thickening" invisible to the set of points alone. Hold onto that; it's where schemes beat classical varieties.
