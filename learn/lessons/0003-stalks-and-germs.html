Stalks & germs as directed colimits

Win: compute a stalk as a colimit and recognize it as a localization $R_{\mathfrak p}$ Time: ~15 min Needs: Lesson 0002; localization Ref: Sheaves cheat-sheet

A sheaf records data on every open set. To study behavior at a point $x$, we zoom in: shrink the open set around $x$ and keep only what survives arbitrarily close to $x$. The result is the stalk $\mathcal F_x$. This is the lesson where your commutative algebra pays a dividend: the stalk of the structure presheaf is exactly the localization $R_{\mathfrak p}$ — no new object, just an old friend wearing a geometric hat.1

1. Stalks: Stacks Project, Tag 0078.

1 · The stalk is a colimit over neighborhoods

Definition — stalk The stalk of a presheaf $\mathcal F$ at $x\in X$ is $$\mathcal F_x \;=\; \operatorname*{colim}_{x\in U}\ \mathcal F(U),$$ the colimit over all open neighborhoods $U\ni x$, ordered by reverse inclusion ($U\ge U'$ iff $U\subseteq U'$), with transition maps the restriction maps of $\mathcal F$.2
2. Defined in the running text of the Stalks section, Tag 0078.
Concrete model — germs $\mathcal F_x=\{(U,s):x\in U,\ s\in\mathcal F(U)\}/\!\sim$, where $(U,s)\sim(U',s')$ iff there is an open $U''\subseteq U\cap U'$ with $x\in U''$ and $s|_{U''}=s'|_{U''}$. The class $s_x$ is the germ of $s$ at $x$: it remembers $s$ only arbitrarily near $x$.
Why "directed" (filtered) is the whole game Any two neighborhoods $U,U'$ of $x$ contain a smaller common one ($U\cap U'$). So any two germs can be represented on a single common $U''$ and then added/multiplied there. That is precisely the directedness that makes localization $S^{-1}R$ work — the stalk colimit and the localization colimit are the same kind of limit-of-shrinking.

2 · The payoff: stalk = localization

Take the structure presheaf on $\operatorname{Spec} R$: $\ \mathcal F(D(f))=R_f$, with restriction $R_f\to R_g$ when $D(g)\subseteq D(f)$. Fix a prime $\mathfrak p$.

The computation to own The neighborhoods $D(f)$ of $\mathfrak p$ are exactly those with $f\notin\mathfrak p$, and $D(f)\cap D(g)=D(fg)$ keeps the system directed. Hence $$\mathcal F_{\mathfrak p}\;=\;\operatorname*{colim}_{f\notin\mathfrak p} R_f\;=\;R_{\mathfrak p},$$ the localization at $\mathfrak p$. Identically for a module: $\widetilde M_{\mathfrak p}=M_{\mathfrak p}$.3
3. This is the load-bearing step of the affine-scheme definition: $\mathcal O_{\operatorname{Spec} R,\,\mathfrak p}=R_{\mathfrak p}$, see Tag 01HV.

Play: shrink to the point

Slide to localize step by step at $\mathfrak p=(x)$ on the affine line $\mathbb A^1=\operatorname{Spec} k[x]$. Each notch removes a point and inverts one more $(x-a)$; the directed colimit is the local ring $k[x]_{(x)}$.

3 · Stalks see the local ring — and locality of sections

  • Every stalk is a local ring. $R_{\mathfrak p}$ has the unique maximal ideal $\mathfrak pR_{\mathfrak p}$. That is the literal reason $\operatorname{Spec} R$ is a locally ringed space — the adjective "locally" refers to the stalks being local rings.4
  • A section is determined by its germs. For a sheaf, the map $\mathcal F(U)\to\prod_{x\in U}\mathcal F_x$ is injective — this is exactly the separatedness clause from Lesson 0002. So two sections agreeing as germs everywhere are equal.5
4. The value of a function $s$ at $x$ lives in the residue field $\kappa(x)=R_{\mathfrak p}/\mathfrak pR_{\mathfrak p}$ — the same residue field from Lesson 0001. The stalk refines that "value" to a whole germ.
